Cambridge applications share the same early UCAS deadline as Oxford, but the process is not a copy of Oxford's. Cambridge has its own college system, its own teaching culture, its own additional application form, and a pool system that can move strong applicants between colleges.

You cannot apply to Oxford and Cambridge in the same UCAS cycle, so the first decision is choosing which university and course structure suits you. Cambridge is often especially distinctive for Natural Sciences, Mathematics, Engineering, Computer Science, Economics, and other subjects where the course structure or admissions test pattern differs from Oxford.

## The Cambridge process at a glance

For 2027 entry, most Cambridge applicants must submit UCAS by **6pm UK time on 15 October 2026**. UCAS applications open in May and can normally be submitted from September. Cambridge then has an additional step: **My Cambridge Application**, due by **6pm UK time on 22 October 2026** for most October applicants.

The usual Cambridge sequence is:

1. Choose your course and decide whether to pick a college or make an open application.
2. Prepare UCAS, including the personal statement and academic reference.
3. Register for any required admissions test, such as ESAT, TMUA, LNAT, or UCAT depending on subject.
4. Submit UCAS by 15 October.
5. Complete My Cambridge Application by 22 October.
6. Submit any transcript or written work if required.
7. Wait for interview shortlisting, usually in November or early December.
8. Attend interviews in December if shortlisted.
9. Receive a decision in January, with some candidates considered through the pool.

The important point: Cambridge sees more than your UCAS statement. The statement still matters, but it sits inside a wider application architecture.

## My Cambridge Application

After you submit UCAS, Cambridge emails you a link to My Cambridge Application. This form checks and expands on your UCAS information. It can include an optional additional personal statement, which is useful if your Cambridge course differs from the other courses in your UCAS choices.

Do not use the optional statement to repeat your UCAS statement. Use it for Cambridge-specific course fit. For example, a Natural Sciences applicant might explain a particular interest in combining biology, chemistry, and mathematics. A student applying for a Cambridge course that is broader or differently structured than their other choices can use this space carefully.

## What Cambridge wants from the personal statement

Cambridge tutors want evidence of serious academic engagement. They are not selecting the student with the most activities. They are selecting students who can cope with supervision: small-group teaching where ideas are tested closely and quickly.

Your statement should therefore show:

- precise subject interest
- reading or exploration beyond school
- evidence that you can think independently
- an honest sense of what you want to understand next
- enough specificity to give interviewers something real to discuss

Cambridge applicants often weaken the statement by trying to cover everything. A better approach is to choose a few academic moments and analyse them properly.

## Admissions tests and subject differences

For 2027 entry, Cambridge lists ESAT for Engineering, Natural Sciences, Chemical Engineering and Biotechnology, and Veterinary Medicine. TMUA is required for Computer Science, Economics, and Mathematics. Law applicants take LNAT, and Medicine applicants take UCAT.

Always check the official Cambridge course page because test requirements and registration deadlines can change. For ESAT and TMUA, Cambridge lists the October sitting for standard applicants, with registration deadlines before the UCAS deadline.

The test should also shape how you write the statement. A Maths, Economics, or Computer Science statement should not sound like an essay-only application; it needs to show mathematical readiness. A Natural Sciences statement should show genuine scientific breadth rather than a loose mixture of unrelated interests.

## Interviews and the supervision style

Cambridge interviews are academic conversations. They are designed to see how you think, not whether you can perform a memorised answer. You may be given unfamiliar problems, data, texts, questions, or scenarios.

Your personal statement can feed that conversation. If you mention a source, claim, project, or idea, assume it may be tested. This is a good thing if the statement is honest: it lets you guide part of the interview towards material you have genuinely thought about.

## The pool

The Cambridge pool is one of the biggest practical differences from Oxford. A college may think you are strong enough for Cambridge but not have space in that subject. In that case, your application can be shared with other colleges. You might receive an offer from another college, and in some cases applicants can still end up with an offer from their original college.

Being pooled is not a failure. It means the application is still live. Students do not need to do anything except wait for the outcome.

## Cambridge personal statement FAQ

### What is My Cambridge Application?

It is Cambridge's additional application form. Most undergraduate applicants complete it after submitting UCAS. It checks details, asks Cambridge-specific questions, and can include an optional additional personal statement.

### Should I use the optional additional personal statement?

Use it only if you have something Cambridge-specific to say. It is useful when the Cambridge course differs from your other UCAS choices. It should not repeat your main personal statement.

### Should my Cambridge statement mention the college?

Usually no. You can choose a college or make an open application, but the personal statement should focus on the academic course rather than college preference.

### What is the Cambridge pool?

The pool lets colleges consider strong applicants who were not offered a place by their original college. A pooled applicant may receive an offer from a different college or, sometimes, still from the original college.
